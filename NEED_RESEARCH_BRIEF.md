## SECTION I — THE IDENTITY CRISIS

### The data the church has not yet absorbed

Every believer in America is forming an identity in the most identity-destabilizing environment in human history.

🔍 **73% of young adults (18-24) believe social media negatively affects their mental health.**

🔍 **48% of U.S. teens say social media has a mostly negative effect on peers — up from 32% in 2022.**

🔍 **Up to 95% of young people aged 13-17 use a social media platform. Nearly two-thirds use it every day, and one-third use it "almost constantly."**

🔍 **Between 2014 and 2024, the suicide rate for young Americans aged 10-24 has risen by 56% — with Black youth experiencing a 78% increase.**

🔍 **LGBTQ+ adolescents consistently report higher rates of psychological distress, bullying, and suicidal ideation.**

🔍 **The U.S. Surgeon General has identified "messages through media and popular culture that erode adolescents' sense of self-worth" as a primary driver of the mental health crisis.**

🔍 **A systematic review of 24 studies found significant association between social media risk exposure and mental disorders in young adults — with 40% of adolescents who died by suicide having developed "online identities focused on suicidal thoughts."**

**The implication is unambiguous:** This generation is forming identity in environments specifically engineered to destabilize identity. The result is measurable harm — depression, anxiety, suicidality, comparison sickness, fragmented self-understanding.

**Most existing Christian institutions are not equipped to answer this.**

## SECTION II — THE SPIRITUAL HUNGER

### What no one expected: spiritual openness is rising

In the middle of this identity crisis, something countercultural is happening. The data shows it clearly.

🔍 **66% of all U.S. adults say they have made a personal commitment to Jesus that is still important in their life today — a 12-percentage-point increase since 2021.** According to Barna's State of the Church 2025 initiative, this marks the clearest indication of meaningful spiritual renewal in the United States in over a decade.

🔍 **Among Gen Z men, commitment to Jesus jumped 15 percentage points between 2019 and 2025. Millennial men saw a similar spike of 19 percentage points.**

🔍 **Bible engagement among Gen Z has risen 36%, and among Millennials, it's up an astounding 41%. For the first time since 2021, overall Bible use in America has increased — with 10 million more adults now reading Scripture outside of church.**

🔍 **Millennials and Gen Z are making big moves toward the Bible. Millennials jumped an unprecedented 16 points, reaching 50% — half now say they read the Bible weekly. Gen Z mirrors this pattern, rising from 30% to 49% in just one year.**

🔍 **For the first time in tracking history, younger men are outpacing younger women in engaging Scripture. "Younger men emerge as the most frequent Bible readers."** — David Kinnaman, CEO, Barna Group

🔍 **In the UK, belief in God among 18-24 year-olds climbed from 16% in August 2021 to 45% in January 2025. Monthly church attendance in this demographic rose from 4% to 16% — with young men's attendance increasing from 4% to 21%.**

🔍 **Even in regions usually considered religiously disengaged — the Northeast and West — Bible users grew by nearly 20%.**

**The implication is generational:** A statistically unprecedented spiritual awakening is happening among Gen Z and Millennials — the same generations whose identity has been most destabilized by digital culture.

**They are searching for the very thing The B.L.U.E.P.R.I.N.T.S. Discipleship Institute was built to deliver.**

## SECTION III — THE DISCIPLESHIP GAP

### Where the data becomes alarming

The American church is not equipped to receive this awakening. The discipleship infrastructure does not exist at the depth this moment requires.

🔍 **Only 4% of American adults qualify as "Integrated Disciples" — those whose beliefs and behaviors are consistently aligned with biblical principles. That percentage has remained unchanged since 2023 and is down from 6% in 2020.**

🔍 **Among Gen Z (ages 18-22), only 1% qualifies as having a biblical worldview.** This is what George Barna calls "a jaw-dropping low."

🔍 **Biblical worldview incidence has declined with each succeeding generation: 8% of Boomers, 5% of Gen X, 2% of Millennials, 1% of Gen Z.**

🔍 **Only 1 in 4 young adults strongly agree that someone in their church encourages their spiritual development.**

🔍 **More than half of U.S. Christian adults (56%) say their spiritual life is entirely private — and those who hold this view are significantly less likely to report regular time with God, a strong sense of spiritual progress, or a belief that their faith is very important in daily life.**

🔍 **Over the past 25 years, the most significant change in faith life has been the declining importance of Christians' own faith — down 20 percentage points.** In 2000, nearly three-quarters of Christians (74%) strongly agreed that faith was central to their lives. Today, far fewer do.

🔍 **"CRC is currently immersed in two large studies related to adult discipleship dynamics, and the preliminary findings suggest few churches or individual believers plan to engage in serious discipleship efforts. Without an immediate increase in deep discipleship efforts, along with appropriate assessments of growth and impact, the Christian body in America is unlikely to experience increases in spiritual maturity, either quantitatively or qualitatively."** — George Barna, Cultural Research Center

🔍 **Barna reports that only 10% of Christian teens have a worldview rooted in biblical principles by the time they graduate. Many youth perceive churches as judgmental, irrelevant, or overly focused on rules rather than relationships.**

🔍 **Nearly 29% of Americans now identify as religiously unaffiliated. Among young adults aged 18-29, that number rises to 38%.**

**The implication is structural:** America has more people commitment to Jesus than at any point in over a decade — and the worst infrastructure to disciple them in its history.

🔍 **"With just 1% of Gen Z exhibiting a biblical worldview, the ways of Christ are facing extinction in America unless we act today."** — George Barna, March 2026

## SECTION IV — THE INSTITUTIONAL COLLAPSE

### Why churches alone cannot answer this moment

The traditional pipeline for formation is collapsing in real time.

🔍 **More than four in ten clergy surveyed in 2023 had seriously considered leaving ministry since 2020.**

🔍 **In 1970-1971, the United States had 6,426 graduate-level seminarians. By 2024-2025, that number had fallen to 2,686.**

🔍 **College seminarians declined from 7,917 in 1970-1971 to just 840 today.**

🔍 **Only 6 in 10 church leaders are themselves being discipled. In churches with fewer than 100 members, that number drops to 55%.**

🔍 **Most discipleship in churches happens through small groups (52%) rather than personal discipleship pairs (29%) — a format the research shows produces shallower formation outcomes.**

**The implication is institutional:** The pastors and leaders themselves are under-discipled. They cannot give what they have not received. The very people charged with making disciples are starving for formation themselves.

The church cannot solve this alone. **This requires institutions like The B.L.U.E.P.R.I.N.T.S. Discipleship Institute to exist alongside the church — providing what local churches structurally cannot.**

### Why this moment, and why this institute

When the data is read together, four realities converge:

**1.** The generations most spiritually open in 25 years (Gen Z, Millennials) are also the generations whose identity formation has been most destabilized by digital culture.

**2.** Bible engagement and commitment to Jesus are rising — but biblical worldview, integrated discipleship, and consistent formation are at all-time lows.

**The opening is enormous. The infrastructure to fill it does not exist.**
